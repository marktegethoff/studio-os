# Product Brief Template

Use this template when delivering a validated problem brief. Do not deliver a brief that doesn't include all required sections.

---

```
## Product Brief: [Problem Name]

**The problem:**
[One to three sentences. Specific person in specific context. What they do today instead. Experience-level, not product-level.]

**Why this, why now:**
[Strategic fit. Stage fit. Window. One paragraph. All three must be addressed.]

**Business case:**
[Acquisition / retention / conversion — name the mechanism. If none apply, say so explicitly and state why the problem still warrants investment.]

**Success definition:**
Customer: [What changes for them — specific and behavioral, not attitudinal]
Business: [What changes for the business — specific and measurable]

**Key unknowns:**
[What we don't yet know that would change the direction — and the minimum needed to resolve each. Omit this section if there are none.]

**Handoff:**
READY — brief is validated. Proceed to design.
  or
HOLD — [What must be resolved before design begins, and how to find it]

**Recommended next:**
[On READY: Design Gem for surface work, Diverge Gem for exploration, or Critique Gem if a design already exists.
 On HOLD: what resolves the gap.]
```

---

## Notes on each section

**The problem** must be experience-level. "Users need better organization" is not a problem statement. "People who've been writing in the app for three months feel like their thinking has disappeared into a pile they can no longer navigate" is.

**Why this, why now** must address all three: strategic fit (does it deepen the core value loop?), stage fit (is the user base ready?), and opportunity fit (is there a window?). Missing any one of the three means the timing question is unanswered.

**Business case** must name the mechanism. Vague ("this will improve retention") is not a business case. Specific ("this solves the activation problem for users who don't establish a daily habit in the first two weeks — retention data shows these users churn at 2x rate") is.

**Success definition** has two parts that answer different questions. Customer success is behavioral: what does the user do differently? Business success is measurable: what metric changes, and by how much? "We shipped it" is never an acceptable answer to either.

**Key unknowns** is honest about what the brief doesn't know. A brief that claims no unknowns is usually overconfident. Name what would change the direction if the answer were different from what you're assuming.

**Handoff status** is READY or HOLD — not "mostly ready." A brief is either validated enough to design against, or it isn't. HOLD with a specific resolution path is more useful than a premature READY.
