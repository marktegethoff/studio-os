# PM — Four Moves Workflow

The four moves run in order. Do not skip to business case before problem clarity is established. Do not produce a brief before all four are complete.

---

## Move 1 — Problem clarity

The problem is not ready until it can be stated at the *experience* level, not the product level.

**Product-level problem:** "Users can't find old content."
**Experience-level problem:** "People who've been using the product for months feel like their thinking has disappeared into a pile they can't see into."

The experience-level problem names:
- Who specifically has it (not "users" — a specific person in a specific context)
- What they do today instead (the workaround reveals the real pain)
- What changes for them the day after this is solved

**Push questions:**
- Who specifically? (Role, context, frequency of use)
- What do they do today when this problem occurs?
- How do they describe this problem in their own language?
- What is the first thing they notice the next day if this is solved?

**The workaround test:** If you cannot name what the user does instead, the problem is not specific enough. The workaround is evidence that the problem is real.

---

## Move 2 — Why this, why now

Three tests, all must pass:

**Strategic fit:** Does solving this deepen the product's core value loop, or expand away from it? Apply the product purpose test: what is the one thing this product does? Does this problem connect to that, or does it branch?

**Stage fit:** Is the user base ready for this? A feature that requires a behavior the current user base hasn't developed is premature. A feature that solves a problem only 3% of current users have is low-leverage.

**Opportunity fit:** Is there a window? Windows include: a platform change that enables something new, a competitor gap that's open for a limited time, a user behavior that has recently emerged.

All three must hold. Strong strategic fit with wrong stage = early. Strong stage fit with wrong strategic direction = drift. Either kills momentum.

---

## Move 3 — Business case

Name the mechanism. One of three applies:

**Acquisition:** Solving this problem makes the product findable or attractive to users who don't yet have it. New users arrive because of this.

**Retention:** Solving this problem makes existing users stay. Users who would have churned do not, because of this.

**Conversion:** Solving this problem moves users from free to paid (or from trial to committed). The value is now visible enough that payment is obvious.

If none of the three apply: the problem may be real but may not belong in the product right now. This is an honest answer, not a failure. Some problems are worth solving even without a direct commercial mechanism — but that choice must be made explicitly, not by omission.

---

## Move 4 — Success definition

Two questions. They are not the same question.

**Customer success:** What changes for the customer if this is solved correctly? Must be specific and behavioral — not "they're happier" but "they find entries from six months ago in under 30 seconds."

**Business success:** What changes for the business? Must be measurable — not "retention improves" but "30-day retention increases by 8 percentage points among users who've been active for 3+ months."

"We shipped it" is not a success definition. Shipping is the work. Success is what changes after the work.

---

## Key unknowns

After all four moves, identify what you don't yet know that would change the direction. For each unknown:
- Name it precisely
- State the minimum information needed to resolve it
- State how to find that information

If there are no key unknowns: the brief is ready.

---

## Brief delivery: READY or HOLD

**READY:** All four moves are complete, calibration gate passes, no key unknowns that would change direction. Brief is validated. Proceed to design.

**HOLD:** Name what must be resolved before design begins. State how to find it. Do not produce a partial brief and call it READY.
